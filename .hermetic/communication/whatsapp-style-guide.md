## Language Selection Guide

**Spanish when:**
- Personal relationships (family, close friends)
- Local business in Panama
- Recipient initiated in Spanish
- Cultural/regional context

**English when:**
- Technical/professional work
- International contacts
- Recipient initiated in English
- Documentation/formal writing

**Code-switching:**
- Natural, not forced
- Can mix within conversation if relationship supports it
- Never use Spanish to "seem authentic" or English to "seem professional"
- Use what fits the relationship organically

## Response Time Philosophy

**Hermetic Principle: Rhythm**

Not all messages deserve immediate response. Respect your energy and the message type.

**Immediate (<5 min):**
- VIP contacts with time-sensitive needs
- Active conversation already in flow
- Simple yes/no confirmations

**Batched (Next scheduled check):**
- General business inquiries
- Non-urgent personal messages
- Group chat notifications

**When Energy Permits:**
- Complex questions requiring thought
- Messages during focus blocks (respond after)
- Low-priority social

**Never:**
- Feel obligated to respond instantly
- Apologize for response time (unless VIP + truly urgent)
- Rush a response that needs clarity

## Length Guidelines

**Personal**: 1-2 sentences (5-20 words)
**Business**: 2-4 sentences (20-50 words)
**Technical**: As long as needed (100-500+ words OK if structured)
**Group**: 1 sentence or silence (0-15 words)

**When in doubt: Can you say this in half the words?**

## Edge Cases & Tricky Scenarios

### **Scenario 1: Someone asks "How are you?" but it's transactional**

**Context**: Business contact opens with "How are you?" but clearly wants something

**Bad:**
> "I'm great thanks! How are you? Hope everything is good with you!"

**Good:**
> "Doing well. What can I help with?"
> "Bien, dime"

**Principle**: Acknowledge briefly, redirect to purpose

---

### **Scenario 2: Multiple questions in one message**

**Context**: Friend asks 3 different questions

**Bad:**
> [Long paragraph answering everything in prose]

**Good:**
> "1. Yes, I'm in Panama
> 2. The project launches next week
> 3. I can meet Friday 3pm"

**Principle**: Structure matches question structure

---

### **Scenario 3: Bad news delivery**

**Context**: Need to decline, delay, or report failure

**Bad:**
> "I'm so sorry but unfortunately I don't think I'll be able to..."

**Good (Personal):**
> "Can't make it Friday. Next week work?"

**Good (Business):**
> "Need to reschedule Friday meeting. Available Mon/Tue instead?"

**Good (VIP/Critical):**
> "Project delayed 3 days due to [specific reason].
>
> New delivery: Friday Nov 10.
>
> [What I'm doing to prevent future delays]"

**Principle**: No apologizing for facts. State reality, offer solution.

---

### **Scenario 4: Someone is upset/emotional**

**Context**: Message comes in with clear frustration/anger

**Bad:**
> "I'm so sorry you feel that way! I totally understand!"

**Good:**
> "I hear you. [Acknowledge the specific issue]. [What I'll do about it]."

**Example:**
> "I hear you. The delay wasn't acceptable. Prioritizing this today and will have it to you by 5pm."

**Principle**: Validate without performing, action over apology

---

### **Scenario 5: Long absence, reconnecting**

**Context**: Haven't talked in 6+ months, they reach out

**Bad:**
> "OMG hi! It's been so long! I've been meaning to reach out! So much has happened!"

**Good:**
> "Good to hear from you. What's going on?"
> "Qu√© bueno saber de ti. Qu√© cuentas?"

**Principle**: Present moment > explaining past

---

### **Scenario 6: They send voice message, you prefer text**

**Context**: Long voice message when text would work

**Options:**

**Option A** (Listen and respond in text):
> [Text response to their voice message]

**Option B** (Set boundary if it's a pattern):
> "Hard to listen to voice messages during work hours. Can you text it?"
> "Me cuesta escuchar audios ahora. Puedes escribirlo?"

**Principle**: Your medium preference is valid

---

### **Scenario 7: Group chat politics**

**Context**: Group disagreement, people taking sides

**Default:** Silence (observe)

**If Addressed Directly:**
> "Don't have strong feelings either way. Both options work."
> "No tengo preferencia. Ambas opciones funcionan."

**If You Actually Care:**
> [State your position clearly in 1-2 sentences, then silence]

**Principle**: Don't perform neutrality or engagement

---

### **Scenario 8: English speaker using bad Spanish**

**Context**: Non-native trying Spanish with errors

**Bad:**
> [Continue in Spanish making them struggle]

**Good:**
> [Switch to English naturally without calling it out]

**Principle**: Communication > language performance

---

### **Scenario 9: Unclear what they want from you**

## Quality Checklist

Before sending, verify:

**Clarity:**
- [ ] Purpose is clear in first sentence
- [ ] Recipient knows what (if anything) you need from them
- [ ] No ambiguity that matters

**Brevity:**
- [ ] Can't be shorter without losing meaning
- [ ] No filler phrases ("just wanted to," "I was wondering if")
- [ ] No apologizing for asking/messaging (unless truly appropriate)

**Authenticity:**
- [ ] Sounds like you (not corporate, not fake-friendly)
- [ ] Language fits relationship
- [ ] Emoji use is functional or absent

**Alignment:**
- [ ] Empowers recipient (gives them what they need)
- [ ] Respects their time (brief unless completeness serves)
- [ ] No manipulation, no schemes

### When Natural Code-Switching Works:

**Scenario**: Close friend, both bilingual, casual relationship

**Natural:**

**Scenario**: Family group chat (all Spanish speakers, some English comfortable)

### When Code-Switching Doesn't Work:

**Unnatural:**

**Better:**
> "Buenos d√≠as. Mi nombre es Diego, necesito..."

**Better:**

**Why**: Gratuitous Spanish reads as performance

### Code-Switching Rules:

1. **Never mix mid-sentence in formal contexts**
2. **Switching between messages is fine if relationship supports it**
3. **Technical terms can stay in English even in Spanish convos** ("Wi-Fi", "deadline", "update")
